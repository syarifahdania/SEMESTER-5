## 🗄️ Database Programming · SECP3623

Building on the relational design foundations of Year 2, this course moved into the advanced programming layer of database work — stored procedures, triggers, views, transactions, and the art of query optimisation. It is where database knowledge became database engineering.

The performance dimension of this course was particularly illuminating. The same logical query, written differently or executed against different indexes, can perform orders of magnitude apart. Learning to read query execution plans and reason about index selection turned SQL from a retrieval language into an engineering discipline. I now want to extend this expertise to cloud data warehousing environments — Snowflake, BigQuery, and AWS Redshift — where these same principles apply at the scale that modern data engineering demands.

## 🏗️ Enterprise Systems Design and Modelling · WBL · SECP3744

This course elevated my architectural thinking from individual applications to organisation-wide systems. Enterprise architecture, UML modelling, business process design, ERP integration patterns, and service-oriented architecture formed a curriculum that required thinking at a scale I had not encountered before.

The WBL component was where the complexity became real. Designing systems for organisations with thousands of users, complex module interdependencies, and legacy infrastructure is fundamentally different from building a standalone application — it involves negotiation, compromise, and a careful understanding of the human and organisational constraints that are just as real as the technical ones. This course shaped how I think about systems not as technical objects but as socio-technical ones, and that perspective has made me a more complete designer.

## 🗣️ Professional Communication Skills · UHLB3132

By Year 3, I had a body of real work to communicate — research, system designs, data projects — which made this advanced communication course feel immediately applicable rather than preparatory. The focus on executive-level presentations, research documentation, and high-stakes written communication addressed challenges I was already navigating in my academic and project work.

The iterative feedback cycle — draft, present, receive critique, revise — accelerated my development more effectively than any single lesson. I became significantly more comfortable presenting technical work to mixed audiences and more disciplined about structuring written work so that its argument is clear before its detail. Communication is the surface through which all technical work is ultimately judged, and investing in it seriously has been one of the most professionally consequential decisions of my degree.

## 🤖 Artificial Intelligence · SECJ3553

Artificial intelligence has become the defining subject of the technology era I am entering, and this course provided a rigorous grounding in both classical and contemporary approaches — search algorithms, knowledge representation, expert systems, machine learning fundamentals, neural networks, and the ethics of AI deployment.

Moving from rule-based systems to probabilistic learning revealed something important: much of what we call intelligence is pattern recognition at scale, operating on data of a quality and quantity that humans cannot process manually. That understanding is directly connected to data engineering — the systems I build are the infrastructure that makes AI possible or impossible. The ethics component of the course was not peripheral. Understanding the societal implications of automated decision-making is, in my view, a professional obligation for anyone working in this field.

## 🀄 Mandarin Language I · UHLC1112

Mandarin was a different kind of challenge from anything else in my degree — there were no algorithms, no frameworks, and no debugging tools. Just patient, repetitive practice and the humility to be a genuine beginner. The course covered pinyin, foundational vocabulary, basic grammar, and the tonal system that makes Mandarin phonetically distinctive from any language I had encountered before.

Beyond the language itself, this course expanded my cultural awareness in ways that feel professionally relevant. China's technology sector is a major force in global computing, and the ability to engage — even at a basic level — across that cultural and linguistic boundary is meaningful. Language learning also taught me something about the nature of skill acquisition: consistency and patience matter more than intensity, and that lesson applies well beyond the classroom.

## 🧠 Social Psychology · SHMY1033

Social Psychology explored the invisible forces that shape human behaviour in group contexts — social influence, cognitive biases, conformity, persuasion, and interpersonal dynamics. It was one of the most directly applicable courses to my professional life, despite having no technical content.

Understanding cognitive biases — anchoring, confirmation bias, groupthink, the bystander effect — changed how I evaluate my own reasoning and how I participate in team decisions. I now recognise the group dynamics that can lead technically capable teams to make poor decisions, and I approach collaborative work with more deliberate awareness of those forces. For system design, the insight that users do not behave the way designers assume they will — because of predictable, well-documented cognitive patterns — has made me a more realistic and therefore more effective designer.
